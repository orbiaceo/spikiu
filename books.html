Chapter 3
A Coffee on Gran Vía

Madrid, 10:30 in the morning. Carlos and Ana are sitting in a small café near the Telefónica building. The street is already full of people.

Ana stirs her coffee slowly. She looks out onto the street and thinks about yesterday's meeting. Something was not quite right — but she cannot say exactly what.

Carlos notices her silence.

Carlos: — ¿En qué piensas?

Ana: — En la reunión de ayer. Creo que hay algo que no entendí bien.

Carlos: — ¿Qué parte exactamente?

Ana: — Cuando hablaron de los plazos. No estoy segura si acordaron tres semanas o tres meses.

Carlos laughs quietly. It is a warm laugh — not mocking, just understanding.

Carlos: — Tres semanas. Pero no te preocupes — yo también me confundo a veces.

Ana: — ¿De verdad?

Carlos: — De verdad. El español de los negocios tiene sus propias reglas.

Ana smiles. She takes a sip of her coffee and looks at the street again. This time with a little more calm.

Ana: — ¿Puedes explicarme cómo funciona exactamente?

Carlos: — Con mucho gusto. Pero primero — ¿otro café?

The Subjuntivo Present

The Subjuntivo is the part of Spanish that makes Germans nervous — and for good reason. In German, you say "Ich hoffe, dass er kommt." In Spanish, "comes" turns into something else entirely. But don't panic. Once you understand the logic, it is actually quite satisfying.

🐾 Think of the Subjuntivo as Spikiu's way of saying: "I'm not sure this will happen, but I really want it to." Like hoping for a good coffee in an airport.

When do you use it?

Whenever you express a wish, a doubt, an emotion, or a recommendation — you use the Subjuntivo in the second part of the sentence.

The pattern

Espero que + Subjuntivo

Quiero que + Subjuntivo

Es importante que + Subjuntivo

No creo que + Subjuntivo

How to form it:

Take the yo form of the present tense → remove the -o → add the new endings.

Real examples from Ana's conversations:

From Chapter 3

"Espero que podamos acordar los plazos."

→ I hope we can agree on the deadlines.

"No creo que sea tan difícil."

→ I don't think it is that difficult.

🐾 The irregular verbs in Subjuntivo? Yes, they exist. But here's the secret: the most common ones — ser, estar, ir, haber — you will learn naturally from conversations. Spikiu will bring them back at the right moment.
